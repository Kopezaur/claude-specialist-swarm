2. What changed inside each specialist's brief

Commit 2ab9c39 rewrote all four specialist system prompts in create_specialists.py. They went from generic templated briefs to fully Acme-RFP-aware operators with explicit deliverable lengths, structured output formats, and a final recommendation field.

Figure 2 — Output-section count in each specialist's system prompt, before vs. after 2ab9c39. Every brief roughly doubled, all gained an explicit final recommendation step.

Pricing Specialist rewritten

What's new

  • Now names Acme's four commercial demands explicitly: 35% discount, Net 90, 5-year no-escalator, MFN
  • Must classify each as non-starter / negotiable / acceptable with exact counter-language
  • Output grew from 4 to 7 numbered sections, ~500 words
  • Closes with a deal recommendation: bid aggressively / bid with conditions / consider walking away

Why it matters

Previously the specialist could return generic "we'd offer Enterprise tier" prose. Now it cannot dodge the four hard demands — the brief forces an answer on each one.

Legal Reviewer rewritten

What's new

  • Must work through all ten checklist categories — including silence-as-deviation
  • Mandatory format per flag: ITEM N · CATEGORY · RFP says · Severity · Why · Counter
  • Mandatory Aggregate Risk Matrix at end with totals + overall HIGH/MED/LOW + bid/no-bid
  • Output ~500 words

Why it matters

The coordinator can now reconcile pricing-vs-legal conflicts mechanically by reading the risk matrix, rather than scanning prose for blockers.

Technical Fit Specialist rewritten

What's new

  • Five Acme-specific focal points: 80K events/sec, Power BI, EU residency, 99.99% SLA, 2027 Teradata deadline
  • Must estimate weeks to first production workload and weeks to full migration
  • Must explicitly state whether we beat the 2027 deadline
  • Output ~400 words

Why it matters

Adds a hard time-to-value commitment the coordinator can drop straight into the implementation plan.

Competitive Intel Analyst rewritten + upgraded model

What's new

  • Model upgraded claude-haiku-4-5 → claude-sonnet-4-6 (named 4-way race; quality > cost)
  • Brief now names all four named competitors (Databricks, Snowflake, Fabric, unnamed regional)
  • Must rank threats and pick a primary target to displace
  • Win-probability call required: HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW

Why it matters

Previously could "guess" who else was in the deal. Now it analyses the disclosed shortlist and commits to a primary target — which the coordinator uses to focus the proposal's competitive narrative.

6. Net takeaways

  1. The swarm went from generic to deal-specific. Specialists now name Acme's actual numbers (35%, Net 90, 80K events/sec, 2027 Teradata deadline) in their briefs — they can't return vague answers.
  2. Outputs are now machine-reconcilable. Aggregate Risk Matrix, Threat Ranking, deal recommendation pills mean the coordinator can resolve conflicts on a structured field rather than parsing prose.
  3. Conflict is no longer silent. Coordinator must escalate Legal-vs-Pricing disagreements rather than pick a side. The biggest behavioural change.
  4. Deliverable branding is codified. The new docx-creation skill removes ambiguity about how Hogwarts-facing documents look — and was exercised end-to-end the same afternoon.
  5. One model was upgraded. Competitive Intel moved Haiku → Sonnet: a deliberate cost-vs-quality call for a deal with a named four-way race.
